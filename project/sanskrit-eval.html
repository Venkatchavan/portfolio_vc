SanskritEval — LM Linguistic Benchmark

Probing Sandhi and Case Generalization in Language Models

View on GitHub

Project Overview

A benchmark suite for evaluating how well language models handle Sanskrit-specific linguistic phenomena — sandhi (phonological fusion at word boundaries) and morphological case agreement (8 cases × 3 numbers × 3 genders). Built on 701 verses from the Bhagavad Gita, the benchmark includes sandhi segmentation (701 silver + 200 gold examples) and morphological acceptability contrast sets (500 minimal pairs). Evaluates 5 transformer models (mBERT, XLM-R Base/Large, IndicBERT, MuRIL) to probe whether language models learn genuine abstraction or surface-level patterns.

Technologies Used

  • Python 3.10+ & PyTorch
  • Hugging Face Transformers
  • mBERT & XLM-R (Base/Large)
  • IndicBERT & MuRIL
  • Conda & Makefile
  • JSONL Data Formats

Key Features

  • Sandhi segmentation: word boundary detection in fused Sanskrit text
  • Morphological acceptability: 500 contrast pairs (case/number)
  • 5 transformer models: mBERT, XLM-R, IndicBERT, MuRIL (110M-550M params)
  • Rule-based baseline achieving 1.000 F1 score
  • Automated benchmark runner with plots and CSV export

Technical Challenges

  • Sanskrit morphology: 8 cases × 3 numbers × 3 genders combinatorics
  • Creating high-quality gold data requiring expert annotation
  • Evaluating genuine abstraction vs. surface patterns
  • Fair evaluation across models with different pretraining corpora

Dataset & Research

  • Source corpus: 701 verses from Bhagavad Gita
  • Sandhi: 701 silver training + 200 gold test examples
  • Morphology: 500 contrast pairs (333 case + 167 number)
  • Paper-style report with 6 phases of research
  • 4 visualization plot types for results analysis

Results & Impact

The benchmark reveals critical insights about how language models handle low-resource languages with complex morphology. The rule-based baseline achieves perfect 1.000 F1 on sandhi segmentation, while transformer models are expected to achieve 60-85% accuracy on morphological acceptability — probing the gap between memorization and genuine linguistic abstraction. Sanskrit serves as a critical test case representing challenges faced by many of the world's 7,000+ languages.
